## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

Identify and describe survey features:

```
1. Sample type: survey with a cross-sectional design (i.e., stratified with probability sampling)
2. Sample size: 16760 (41.9% of ~40000 sample sent out were completed/responded)
3. Target population: individuals aged 15 years and older living in the ten Canadian provinces, excluding full-time residents of institutions.
4. Sampling frame: combination of two sources: (a) landline and cellular telephone numbers from the Census and (b) addresses from Statistics Canada's dwelling frame (e.g. cross-listings of telephone number(s) associated with a particular address)
5. Survey mode(s): an electronic questionnaire or through CATI (computer assisted telephone interviewing) 
6. Timeline: Data collection occured from 4th September 2018 to 28 December 2018.
7. Response rate: 41.9% response rate
8. Weights: A basic weighting factor for analyis at the individual level, they used the weighting favtor WGHT_PER (e.g., The number of persons represented by a given respondent). Further, bootstrap weights were created for design-based variance estimation. Characteristic-based adjustments (by weighting) were also made to estimates based on the survey data (since they have monthly independent estimates for various age-sex groups by province).
9. Data processing: Processing involved the Social Survey Processing Environment set of generalized processing steps and utilities to allow subject matter and survey support staff to specify and run the processing of the survey efficiently and accurately. This invovles a structured environment that monitors the processing of data, ensuring quality while following agreed-upon protocol.
10. Cleaning, imputation, etc. 

Cleaning (editing) occured automatically and manually at various micro and macro stages. The Computer-Assisted Telephone Interview (CATI) system has build-in functions to edit and update codes for each question. All survey records were also subject to edits throughout an interview, such that out-of-rage values could be corrected in real time with the respondent, and if not resolved, forwarded to head office to resolve on their level. Check were made by the head office. 

Imputations, in all but few cases, were made using donor records identified through a score function, and involved comparisons between recipient and donor records. Imputation involves 9-steps, starting with imputing personal income and family income, followed by imputation of other formal volunteering variables, informal volunteering variables, donation variables and soliciation methods in the master file. 

11. Sources of error: Sampling and non-sampling errors persist regardless of error reduction efforts in the survey design. Considering that the survey data is based on a sample from the population (i.e., a subset of the entire population), results will be different. Bootstrapping was conducted to mitigate this. Non-sampling errors include imperfect coverage (e.g., difference between target and surveyed population. The study excluded households without telephones, so the overall result would still be different from if they were included). Other non-sampling errors include response errors (by participats) and processing errors (by the researchers).
12. Limitations, known biases, etc: A natural bias is non-response. The researchers reduced such a bias by making adjustments to the survey weights. For instance, they a;sp extracted basic characteristics of non-responding households (taking for example. income and household composition) to model and adjust for non-response.
13. Link to documentation and any additional sources used: https://www23.statcan.gc.ca/imdb/p2SV.pl?Function=getSurvey&SDDS=4430
```
